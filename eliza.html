How it works: ELIZA scans your input for keywords ranked by importance. When found, it applies pattern matching to extract pieces of your sentence, flips pronouns (I/you, my/your), and slots them into pre-written response templates. No understanding occurs. It's a parlor trick from 1966.
Why it matters: ELIZA revealed how easily humans attribute understanding to machines. Weizenbaum's own secretary asked to be left alone with it. This "ELIZA effect" shaped decades of AI research and ethics. Modern chatbots use neural networks instead of pattern matching, but the core insight remains: convincing isn't the same as understanding.
